Sinusoidal Function- Bench by Garden

Parent function: f(x)= sin(x), g(x)=x, h(x)= sin(x)+x
Transformation function: h(x)= -0.55 sin [-1.19(x-0.85)] - 5.3 + (0.2x + 0.19)

Transformations

I really appreciate the outdoor gardens with benches Sunway put around the school, its really relaing and a way to distress. For this function, it is a combination of a linear function and sine function. At the beginning, I had trouble aligning the full sine function on the bench as from the angle I took the picture, the position of the function was slightly tilted. I had thought to either rotate the image or to restrict the domain and range of the graph to the section it could only align. However, I managed to find a way to rotate the graph instead. As I could freely adjust the angle of a linear graph, combining the sine with the linear function would allow me to rotate the sine function. For the sine function, as the length of that section of the bench was quite short, the amplitude would be a small value. I gave it an amplitude of 0.55, having a vertical stretch of factor 0.55. I know the value of k, the factor within the function, means the amount of graphs present when x=2pie. I used the max and min points of the graph to estimate the start and end point of one period. I estimated that the graph slightly started over at the positive x-axis, and therefore would end a little before it hit 2pie (6.28), and base on the length and width of the sine wave on the bench, I estimated that in 6.28 units, only one graph and a quarter more would be able to fit. So, I knew my k would be in the range of 1.25 and below. K ended up being a value of 1.19, meaning 1.19 cycles is within 2pie, about one full period plus a little more. I calculated the period by dividing 2pie with 1.19. With a period of 5.28, it has a horizontal stretch of factor 1/1.19. Based on my estimated starting and ending points through my max and min, I estimated the graph to start a little to the right of the x-axis. Therefore, it underwent a transformation of 0.85 units to the right. Lastly, from y=0, the image was around five units down and so I found the translation of 5.3 units downwards. The negative from the amplitude and the factor within the function, k, cancels each other out, having no effect on the graph. Moreover, the graph starts from the left instead of the right of the x-axis, and the bottom of the y-axis, meaning it would be a positive sine graph. For the linear function, as the graph didn’t lean too much, it will have a slight gradient of 0.2 and only went upwards by 0.19 units.

Domain and Range

The domain for the parent function is {x∈R}, and the range is {y∈R, -1≤y≤1}. I thought of discussing the end behaviors of the graph for when x approaches positive and negative infinity, however, there’s no specific point for the end behavior of a sine graph function. We also cannot say y approaches infinity because it doesn’t, the amplitude of the sine function doesn’t increase, therefore it cannot approach infinity. As for the domain of the transformation function, it is {x∈R, -3.87≤x≤0}, and the range being {y∈R, -5.78≤x≤-5.16}. I restricted the domain between the start of the white string intersecting with the bench and when the red brick starts to intersect with the bench. I found the range through the maximum and minimum point of the sine wave on the bench.

Logarithmic Function- Run Club

Parent function: f(x)= log(x)
Transformation function: g(x)= 1.5 log(x+1.5) +1.7

Transformations

I chose this picture as I really enjoy running this semester, all because I joined the run club and managed to improve and run my first 10k! His head was slightly less curvier than the parent function so I first applied a vertical stretch of factor 1.5 to match the shape of his head. I then shifted the graph 1.5 units to the left as his head crossed slightly to the negative side of the x-axis and lifted the graph by 1.7 units upwards to correspond to the top of his head that’s on the positive side of the y-axis. As it’s been shifted to the left by 1.5 units, it’s vertical asymptote is at x=1.5, but it doesn’t affect my graph and I restricted my domain before -1.5.

Domain and Range

The domain for the parent function is {x∈R, x>0} where x cannot equal 0 as the graph will never touch that point. The range is {y∈R}. As x approaches positive infinity, y will also approach positive infinity, but very slowly. Example, log(10)=1, log(1000)=2. x also cannot approach negative infinity as you cannot log a negative number. However, in my transformed function, without the domain, my log function can cross the negative x-axis as my graph has an asymptote of -1.5 but cannot approach infinity. I set a domain of {0.75≥x≥-1.275}, between the left of his glasses and the start of his right eye. The range is {y∈R, 0.73≤y≤2.23}, between the third visible white line on the track, counting upwards, and the maximum point of Ms. Annie’s right eye.

Linear reciprocal- High school friends

Parent function: f(x)= 1/x
Transformation function: g(x)=-0.9/[1.3(x-2.4)] + 3.1

Transformations

I chose this picture as I'm really grateful for the high school friends I met, and this was when we went to Mcdonald's for their buffet during raya. I first knew it had to do a reflection on the y-axis as the higher side of the graph was on the left, and so I added a negative sign. I then tried to match the gap between the two heads, and as I know the value in charge of the transformations of y, which is represented as a, would create a bigger gap as the number increases, I knew to start of with a smaller value. I got to a value of 0.9, meaning it did a vertical compression of 9/10. I then tried to tighten the gap even more by giving it a horizontal compression of factor 1/1.3. I did this while knowing that if I increase this value, the gap would become smaller as when I equate 1.3x to x, it’ll be divided, meaning x=1/1.3. As the two heads were over at the right of the x-axis, I knew d, the horizontal translation of the graph, would be a negative value. I tried to estimate the number located at the midpoint between both heads as I knew at point (0,0) the reciprocal graph is split evenly into two graphs facing each other on opposite quadrants. I did trial and error to get a value of 2.4. So I moved the graph to the right by 2.4 units. Lastly, as both heads were located at the positive side of the y-axis, I knew c, the vertical translation of the graph, would be a positive value. I also used the midpoint between both heads to estimate how much the graph moved up by. I got a value of 3.1, meaning the graph moved upwards by 3.1 units.

Domain and Range

The parent function has a vertical asymptote of x=0 and has a horizontal asymptote at y=0. This is because as it’s a reciprocal, as the value of x increases, the value gets smaller and smaller, approaching zero. Therefore, as x is approaching both negative and positive infinity, the number is approaching zero, so, we can just say the horizontal asymptote is y=0. Its domain is {x∈R, x≠0} as the denominator cannot equal zero. If not the whole function would be undefined. The range is {y∈R, y≠0}, for y will never equal zero since no matter how much x increases, the value will forever be approaching zero but never zero. For the domain of the transformation function, I needed to find the points that’s most to the left and most to the right. The point I used most left, was the slight right of the chin of the guy (x=1.75) and the point I used most right was the hairline of the girl (x=3.63). I ended up with a domain of {x∈R, 1.75≤x≤3.63}. For the range, I did the same but used points that were most top and most bottom. The line directly touches the bottom of his ear so I used that as my highest point (4.8), and the line was touching her middle finger, so I used that as my lowest point (1.56). I ended up with a range of {1.56≤y≤4.8}. As mentioned before, the denominator cannot equal zero if not undefined, so we can use that to find our vertical asymptote. I made 1.3(x-2.4)=0, and got x=2.4, making that the vertical asymptote. We can also see it where the graph got moved to the right by 2.4 units, as compared to the parent function where x=0 was the horizontal asymptote, the horizontal asymptote got moved to x=2.4. When the degree of the denominator is bigger than the numerator, y=0 will be its horizontal asymptote as the denominator is growing much faster than the numerator. y will be approaching zero as x is approaching negative/positive infinity.
